# Problem Statement
Document my golden rules of work. Includes my understanding how i do work personally, including pitfalls, tips, tricks, and things to avoid

# Golden Rules of Work

#### 1. Consistent Sleep

Nine hours and on-time is so important. Sometimes I need to pull an all nighter, which is fine, but must be the rare exception. I'm a night owl, so my best work hours are between 11pm - 3am. Sorry Jackson, you'll need to find a way to work outside your best hours. 10:00 pm - 6:30 am should be protected for sleep.

#### 2. Triple Everything 

Project estimation or work estimations of any kinda must be 3x what you initially think, no exceptions.

#### 3. Careful About the Work Backlog

If i don't make consistent contribution to this backlog, I notice my mood starts to get a little depressed. PTO is your safety net. When work starts stacking up and stress is increasing, book PTO immediately. The PTO should be 2 weeks to clear stuck times on the backlog. 

#### 4. Laundry Must Be Done 

If laundry isn't done, something has gone wrong. It's really important that the in-unit laundry is fast. Wash time should be approximately thirty minutes and dry times one hour. On new apartment inspections bring a towel to test out the washer dryer unit. Combined washer dryers are terrible, never accept a unit that uses them

#### 5. No More Work From Home

I realize I work so much more efficiently in-office. It's a struggle to stay focused at home. This is just me personally.

#### 6. Be Professional

Don't take things personally, even if folks come off as rude. Keep your eye on the prize. 

#### 7. It's Not You It's the Environment

If you're having trouble getting work done, it's not because you're lazy. It's because there's certain environments that your brain needs. If you're having trouble, don't try harder, but find an easy environment for that type of work. 

For example, some of this work can be a struggle at work or home, but by changing the environment the work can become as easy a breathing for me

* Technical book: 
    - Go to the movie theater 2 hours earlier and sit on a bench to read until the movie begins
* Research whitepaper (80 pager): 
    - Go to a restaurant that takes a while to prepare food, order, and bring the paper to read
* Technical video (1 hour): 
    - open a game of [colonist.io](https://colonist.io) and watch the video between turns

Certain types of context really help my brain do work and other types are the opposite. Things that help my brain work usually include the following: [^1] 

* low simulating content
* no loud noises
* dialogue focused
* doesn't require constant attention
* generally non-life or death stakes involved

Additionally, I have a hyper addictive brain. If I find some interesting content, I basically have to binge the entire series in order to kick the addiction. Never ever pick up something like WoW.

#### 8. Brain Loading

I've noticed that when context switching between big topics, it takes a few days for my brain to re-adjust to the new topic. I call it loading time. Work this time into the estimates. Prep for the fact that the transition phase will feel slow

#### 9. It's Not on Your Shoulders

You are not responsible for the security of the company. The company is responsible for the security. If the company is hacked, it's not your fault. Fortunately, I've been lucky in that I've never had a big breach during any of my tenures, but even if it happens, it's not on you. You might have a better understanding of the threat since you have an offensive background, but you don't dictate what the company and management decides to prioritize

#### 10. Burn Out Boundaries

You have a tendency help people too much. On the surface this might seem good, however this will eventually lead to long-term trouble and burn out. 

* Don't work at 100% percent, else any additional work will lead to burn out
* When asked to pivot to other work, track what work needs to be placed on hold and ask if that is acceptable
* Any DMs push them to the official team channel
* Slack channels: 
    - typically I'll be the first to respond, but really try to avoid the urge to immediately reply
    - push the slack sherpas, being the sole on-call for months and months is not sustainable 

#### 11. You Write To Much

I fully realize a lot of my slack responses can be way too verbose. But when trying to solve a problem, I write out my thought process in order to figure out my answer. This can lead to literal essays in slack, which I'm sure many folks find annoying. When you solve problem and don't immediately press send. Curated your response and par down the content. 

#### 12. You Read To Quickly

I've noticed that I read people's questions in slack too fast. Often, I don't read the underlying question that is asked or the central concern that is voiced. I'm imposing a new rule, that any slack question must be re-read 3x times. And one of them must be out loud.
